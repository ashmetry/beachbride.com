Planning a destination wedding is one of the most exciting — and occasionally overwhelming — decisions a couple can make. Done right, it creates an experience that you and your guests will talk about for the rest of your lives. Done without proper preparation, it becomes a logistical nightmare.

Destination weddings now account for roughly 25% of US weddings, with about 350,000 taking place worldwide each year. The global market was valued at €42.8 billion in 2024. This guide covers every step — how to choose your location, what things actually cost, how to handle the legal side, and how to find the right team.

## What makes a destination wedding different?

A destination wedding moves your ceremony and celebration to a location that's meaningful, beautiful, or simply far from home. The key difference isn't just geography — it's the *entire experience*.

Destination weddings naturally create a multi-day event. Guests travel together, stay at the same resort, share dinners and excursions. The wedding stops being a single Saturday event and becomes a long weekend or full week. Less formal, more intimate, more memorable.

The tradeoff: not everyone can or will attend. The average destination wedding guest list is 60–70 people — compared to 141 for the average US wedding. That reduction is both a challenge (you may need hard conversations with extended family) and an opportunity (you only celebrate with people who genuinely matter to you).

## Step 1: How do you choose the right destination?

Start by matching your priorities to a destination's strengths rather than picking a place because it looks good on Instagram.

**Legal vs. symbolic ceremony**: Some countries make it easy to legally marry (Mexico, Jamaica, Hawaii). Others make it difficult for foreigners (Indonesia requires symbolic ceremonies only). Decide early whether a legal ceremony at your destination matters, or whether you'd prefer to handle the legal piece at home and hold a celebration abroad.

**Guest accessibility**: Cancun and Punta Cana have excellent direct flight connections from most US cities. Bali and Santorini require connections for most North American guests, adding cost and travel time. If you're inviting elderly relatives or families with young children, accessibility matters more.

**Season and weather**: Every destination has a rainy season. Santorini's shoulder season (May, September, October) offers better light and fewer crowds than midsummer. Cancun's rainy season runs June–October. Check historical weather data for your specific location, not just the country.

**Venue availability**: The world's most iconic venues book 12–18 months out. Check availability before you fall deeply in love with a specific location.

## Step 2: How do you set a realistic budget?

Account for the wedding itself, guest-facing costs you choose to cover, and a 10–15% buffer for surprises.

According to The Knot, the average domestic destination wedding costs $39,000 and international ones average $41,000. But destination wedding packages at all-inclusive resorts average just $6,450–$6,550. The gap between package price and total cost comes from add-ons: upgraded florals, an imported photographer, welcome bags, excursions, and tips.

## Step 3: How do you handle legal requirements?

Research the specific requirements for your destination 6–12 months out. Requirements vary widely and change frequently.

Standard documents most countries require: valid passport, original birth certificate (often apostilled), proof of marital status (divorce decree or death certificate if applicable), and sometimes blood test results.

**Residency requirements by country** vary significantly:
- Italy: 2 days minimum
- Mexico: 3 days
- Thailand: 7 days
- France: 30 days

Some countries (like Indonesia) don't recognize foreign marriages at all, so couples hold symbolic ceremonies there and legally marry at home. This approach — legal marriage at home, symbolic celebration abroad — is increasingly popular because it eliminates paperwork stress entirely.

**Important for LGBTQ+ couples**: Same-sex marriages remain illegal in Jamaica and most Caribbean nations. Research your destination's laws carefully.

Your wedding planner handles all of this. It's one of the most important reasons to hire a local expert rather than navigating foreign bureaucracy alone. Always confirm current requirements with your planner or the country's embassy — laws change.

## Step 4: How do you choose and book your venue?

Book 12–18 months ahead. Before signing anything, confirm three things: what's included in the base package, what the rain backup plan is, and how many weddings they host per day.

Venue types and tradeoffs:

- **All-inclusive resorts**: Bundle most costs into one price. Simpler to coordinate, more predictable budget. Some restrict outside vendors. Properties like [Sandals](https://www.sandals.com/) or [Excellence](https://www.excellenceresorts.com/) in Cancun and Jamaica are well-established options.
- **Private villas/boutique venues**: Full vendor flexibility, more personalized aesthetic, but you're coordinating everything separately.
- **Independent beach venues**: Require permits (typically $150–$450, applied for 3–6 months in advance for peak-season dates).

When evaluating venues: ask about backup indoor space, confirm capacity is accurate (photos mislead), and check whether they host multiple weddings simultaneously.

## Step 5: How do you build your vendor team from afar?

Hire a local wedding planner first, then let them guide the rest.

A local planner knows which venues have infrastructure issues, which vendors consistently deliver, which permits take forever, and how to handle the unexpected. Budget $3,000–$8,000 for full-service planning. Bringing vendors from home significantly increases costs — their flights, hotels, and meals become your responsibility ($2,000–$4,000 in vendor travel on top of creative fees). Local hires are almost always more budget-friendly and better connected.

Book photographers, florists, and hair/makeup 9–12 months out. When vetting remotely, prioritize vendors who share full galleries from past destination weddings and can articulate a backup plan for weather issues.

**A venue's onsite coordinator is not a substitute for a planner.** Resort coordinators manage property-specific logistics only — everything off-site falls outside their scope.

## Step 6: How do you manage guest logistics and travel?

Send save-the-dates 9–12 months before the wedding, and make travel information easy to find and act on. Approximately 75–80% of invited guests attend destination weddings when couples communicate logistics early and clearly.

What your guests need from you:
- **A wedding website** with flight recommendations, hotel options at multiple price points, and visa requirements
- **Group hotel blocks** with negotiated rates (aim for $300–$400/night)
- **A clear RSVP deadline** — 6 months out gives you enough time to finalize headcounts with your venue
- **Practical details** they won't think to ask: Is there shade? Should they bring sunscreen? Is the walk from parking to ceremony accessible?

The further the destination, the more guests will decline. Be gracious about it. Consider livestreaming the ceremony for those who can't make the trip, and think about hosting a casual celebration at home afterward.

## Step 7: How do you plan your wedding weekend timeline?

Structure 2–4 days. Most destination weddings follow this framework:

- **Day before**: Couple arrives for venue walkthroughs, vendor meetings, rehearsal
- **Arrival day**: Guests arrive, welcome dinner or cocktail party
- **Wedding day**: Ceremony and reception
- **Day after**: Farewell brunch, optional group excursion

Not every event needs to be mandatory. Give guests breathing room — they're on vacation. Share the final itinerary 1–2 weeks before arrival through your wedding website and welcome packets at the hotel.

## The biggest mistakes couples make

**Skipping weather research**: Hurricane season in the Caribbean runs June–November. August in Santorini means sweltering heat and crowds. Check seasonal patterns before locking in dates.

**Underestimating hidden costs**: Service charges (10–25%), international taxes (up to VAT), vendor travel fees, and permit costs add 10–20% to budgets that don't account for them. Build the buffer.

**Assuming guests will figure out travel**: They won't. Detailed travel guides prevent confusion and no-shows.

**Trusting venue photos without a walkthrough**: Online images misrepresent space, lighting, and noise levels. Request a live video tour or have your planner visit in person.

**Overloading the itinerary**: Your guests are on vacation. Two mandatory events plus optional activities is the right balance.

## Building your timeline

Working backward from your wedding date:

- **18+ months out**: Book venue and planner (especially for peak-season Santorini, Amalfi, Hawaii)
- **12–18 months out**: Book photographer, set your budget, send save-the-dates
- **9–12 months out**: Negotiate room block, send formal invitations
- **6 months out**: Book remaining vendors, start document collection
- **3 months out**: Submit legal documents, finalize all vendor contracts
- **1–2 months out**: Confirm all vendor details, create wedding day timeline
- **2 weeks out**: Final headcount confirmation, vendor payments, marriage license (if local)

The biggest mistake couples make is underestimating how early venue and planner availability runs out at top destinations. If you're already 12 months from your target date, start making calls today.
